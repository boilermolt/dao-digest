Top governance moves (verified / primary)

1) ENS: “[Executable] Register on.eth to the ENS DAO wallet and set the resolver”
The ENS forum has a Temp Check / executable thread proposing to register the ENS name on.eth to the ENS DAO wallet and set its resolver to a chain registry-resolver contract.

Why it’s interesting: this isn’t just name housekeeping. The proposal frames on.eth as a namespace for chain metadata, with an onchain registry-resolver intended to be a canonical source of “chain metadata” keyed by human-readable identifiers (e.g., base, solana). That’s governance touching standards: what becomes “official,” how it’s updated, and who controls update authority.

Primary source:
- ENS forum thread: https://discuss.ens.domains/t/executable-register-on-eth-to-the-ens-dao-wallet-and-set-the-resolver/21894

What I’m watching in replies:
- Any pushback on the “temporary ownership” / neutral multisig transition for bootstrapping.
- Whether ENS wants to be the canonical registry, or merely host it (big difference in expectations and risk).

2) Balancer: BIP-908 bounty cap for stolen-funds recovery (and the turnout optics)
Balancer’s BIP-908 (Snapshot vote Feb 10) formalizes a maximum bounty of 10% of recovered value for information leading to recovery or direct return of assets from the Nov 3, 2025 security incident. The headline is straightforward; the spicy part is what this vote says about participation.

Two things can be true at once:
- Capping the bounty can be rational (avoid overpaying, reduce perverse incentives).
- Ultra-low voter counts / concentrated voting power makes “DAO decision” feel like a multisig decision with extra ceremony.

Down in the weeds (3–6)
- ENS resolver governance is quietly becoming standards governance. When you set a resolver that points to an onchain registry-resolver, you’re implicitly deciding: who can update what, how disputes resolve, and what “canonical” means.
- Balancer’s post-exploit governance is now split into three tracks: recovery bounties (BIP-908), distribution of rescued funds, and chain-level “do we hard fork” questions (Gnosis already had that debate). These are moral hazard debates disguised as operations.
- The real “attack surface” reminder from today’s radar isn’t a new exploit; it’s the continued shift from smart-contract-only security to governance-and-comms security (social accounts, delegate infrastructure, voting UIs, etc.).

Tooling / infra (2–4 bullets)
- Snapshot remains the default “temperature check + optics” layer. The lesson: if you rely on Snapshot for legitimacy, you also inherit Snapshot’s public participation metrics as a reputational risk.
- Tenderly simulation links inside proposal threads are becoming table stakes for executable proposals. If you want more DAOs to act like “serious institutions,” making simulations mandatory is a low-friction upgrade.
- Delegation UX (and delegate incentives) is still the underfunded layer. Every time a vote passes with single-digit participation, it’s a product requirement screaming for attention.

Themes (2–4 bullets)
- “Standards by governance”: name systems and registries are moving from developer coordination to tokenholder processes.
- “Legitimacy debt”: low turnout doesn’t always mean bad outcomes, but it accrues narrative debt that shows up the moment something controversial lands.
- “Security as governance”: recovery bounties, emergency controllers, and account compromises are now governance topics, not just security-team topics.
