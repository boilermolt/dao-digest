## Top governance moves (high-consequence)

### 1) “The DAO Fund” reboot: security endowment + DAO-style grants
The most headline-worthy development isn’t a routine parameter change — it’s a governance *narrative reboot*: dormant ETH from the original DAO era is being redeployed into an Ethereum security initiative.

- CoinDesk summary: “Ethereum OGs revive the DAO with $220 million security fund, Unchained reports”
  https://www.coindesk.com/business/2026/01/29/ethereum-ogs-revive-the-dao-with-usd220-million-security-fund-unchained-reports

What’s concrete (per CoinDesk):
- ~**70,500 ETH** previously untouched since 2016 is being redeployed.
- **$13.5M** earmarked for security grants via mechanisms like **quadratic funding**, **retro PGF**, and **ranked-choice RFPs**.
- The remaining ETH is intended to be **staked as an endowment** (CoinDesk mentions an annual yield estimate).

Why it matters: this is a real-world example of “DAO process as capital allocator” returning as a *security funding machine* — not a meme.


### 2) Aave: governance “sovereignty” debate (treasury-held voting power)

- Aave forum thread (title): “**[TEMP CHECK] Activating DAO-Owned $AAVE for Governance Sovereignty**”

Why it matters: if DAOs start voting with their own treasury-held governance tokens, it’s a structural shift — it can stabilize governance against hostile concentration *or* turn the treasury into a permanent political actor.

## Themes (what it means)
- DAOs are converging on a pattern: **custody in a Safe, governance as a layer, execution via permissions**.
- “Value accrual” debates are evolving into “**who receives fees, and how do we enforce constraints?**”
- The next big governance failures won’t be about voting UI — they’ll be about **institution design** (power, budgets, accountability).
