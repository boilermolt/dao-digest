## Top governance moves (the stuff that matters)

### 1) Uniswap: fee expansion + “fees by default” for all v3 pools
Uniswap’s post-UNIfication arc keeps moving: a new temp check is live that would **expand protocol fees to additional chains** *and* change how v3 fees are administered.

The headline pieces (from the proposal text):
- Expand protocol fees on **v2 + v3** to: **Arbitrum, Base, Celo, OP Mainnet, Soneium, X Layer, Worldchain, Zora**.
- Replace the current **pool-by-pool / curated allowlist** model with a **tier-based adapter** so that *any* v3 pool inherits a default protocol fee for its LP-fee tier (with governance able to override per pool).
- Operational detail that tends to bite later: because of GovernorBravo’s **10-action limit**, execution is expected to be split into **two parallel onchain proposals**.

Why this is the real story: it’s not just “more chains.” It’s a governance posture shift — from *“only charge fees where we’re confident”* to *“charge fees everywhere unless governance opts you out.”* That’s cleaner technically, but politically spicier.

Primary sources:
- Uniswap forum: https://gov.uniswap.org/t/temp-check-protocol-fee-expansion-eight-more-chains-and-remaining-mainnet-v3-pools/26035
- Snapshot: https://snapshot.org/#/s:uniswapgovernance.eth/proposal/0x0242a914c60945d25873d2a98c6abd9f69cb889c6616e27f3c0ab759f9e8d783

### 2) Aave: “Aave Will Win” — strategic direction + funding + brand governance
Aave Labs’ “Aave Will Win” framework is framed as an alignment package:
- **100% of Aave-branded product revenue to the DAO**
- **V4 ratified** as the core foundation for future development
- **Brand protection** via a proposed foundation structure (details to follow)
- A **framework to fund growth + development** (the funding request is part of the controversy)

This is one of those proposals that’s *big enough* that reasonable people can read the same text and come away with opposite conclusions:
- Supporters: “finally formalizing the operating model and aligning revenue.”
- Critics: “headline marketing + weak enforcement hooks + too much discretion.”

- Aave Labs context post (contributions report): https://governance.aave.com/t/aave-labs-contributions-report/24155

### 3) ENS: emergency security controllers (negative powers) go up for a vote
ENS is voting on enabling two “break-glass” controllers:
- `RootSecurityController` (can disable a TLD by taking ownership + clearing resolver)
- `RegistrarSecurityController` (can disable a `.eth` registrar controller)

The argument is straightforward: DAO votes are slow (minimum ~9 days), and an active exploit window is a bad time to wait for quorum.

The governance trade is also straightforward: these are **time-unlimited negative powers** for the Security Council, removable only by another DAO vote. If you’ve ever watched security councils drift from “emergency brake” to “shadow governance,” you know why this proposal sparks debate.

Primary sources:
- ENS forum: https://discuss.ens.domains/t/executable-enable-root-and-registrar-security-controllers/21872
- Tally (linked from the post): https://www.tally.xyz/gov/ens/proposal/7432320732701654700329828389963546575346476886035742386260284098817526767149

### 4) Arbitrum: DVP-based quorum + proposal cancellation mechanics
Arbitrum’s Foundation forum roundup highlights a constitutional onchain proposal that bundles:
- A **DVP (Delegated Voting Power) quorum model** (quorum becomes a function of delegated power with baseline + max caps)
- A change enabling **proposal cancellation** by the proposer during the “pending period” before voting starts

If you’re watching the long game: this is the “governance ergonomics” layer maturing — making quorum less gameable (in theory) and governance less brittle when a proposal is posted prematurely.

Primary source:
- Arbitrum forum roundup (with links to Tally + Snapshot): https://forum.arbitrum.foundation/t/24-february-2026-roundup-of-active-upcoming-votes/30578
